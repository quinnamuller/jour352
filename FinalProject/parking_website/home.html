“I’ve got tickets up the wazoo,” said Des Towey, a recruiting coordinator for Nebraska football.

Towey has a nine-month non-reserved faculty permit, which costs $432. But the permit doesn’t last him the entire recruiting season, so he bought a temporary, $78 pass.

“Unless I want to walk a mile to work every single day, then that’s pretty much my only option,” Towey said. “They don’t give you a whole lot of other options… As far as I’m concerned, this stuff should be included in your salary.”

Big Ten universities vary greatly in how much they charge resident students, commuters and employees to park. The add-ons of tickets and late fees often reach surprising heights.

Student parking in the Big Ten

At the University of Washington, students living on campus pay more than $1,200 for annual parking permits, while students at Purdue University pay just $150.

Three of the conference’s new, West Coast additions — UW, the University of California, Los Angeles and the University of Southern California— top the list for the most expensive resident permits.

While permit prices are often high, students don’t always splurge on them.

The University of Illinois Parking Department offers a $900 annual permit that students rarely purchase, instead opting for the $143 shuttle lot permit, according to Onkar Gore, a junior studying economics and computer science.

Megan Selmer, a junior criminology major at the University of Maryland, pays $936 annually for her overnight storage parking permit.

“I think it’s definitely high for the lack of options and leeway that you get,” she said.

Still, she said getting the permit made sense, allowing her to drive home for breaks and run errands.

Permits for commuting students and employees

Nearly all Big Ten universities are public, many of which serve large commuter student and employee populations. While many schools have discounts for commuter students, the price for employee permits is typically much greater than that of resident permits.

UW again offers the most expensive faculty parking permit on average at $1107.60 annually — one of just three plans averaging over $1,000. Only Purdue and Rutgers have plans that fall under $300.

Instead of including parking in employee salaries as Towey suggested, many universities use salary-based parking systems, which increase permit prices based on set brackets.

“Tickets up the wazoo”

Forging a parking permit at USC sticks violators with a $999 ticket that is at most $500 at any other school in the conference. And it doubles if unpaid after three weeks.

At Purdue, an invalid permit ticket costs the same as getting a resident student permit — and $50 more than a commuter permit.

The cost and frequency of citations have made the offices that oversee them incredibly unpopular on their respective campuses, according to Towey and Selmer.

“They hate us,” said Liliana Meertens, a freshman who works on the Citation Review Unit for the Department of Transportation Services at Maryland. “...Everyone you know who’s had a car on campus has probably had a citation at some point, and so we don't really have the best public view of us.”

Students also have to move their cars for campus sporting events, making things more “inconvenient,” Selmer said.

At Penn State, prices for citations increase to $100 during these events, putting a strain on students who are already spending more than $600 annually for parking.

“A lot of students will say, ‘Oh, I’m paying, you know, $300 or $400 for one semester of parking…I shouldn’t have to be paying all these tickets,’” Meertens said.
